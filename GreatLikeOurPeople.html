El Salvador Great Like Our People (2017)

Short Description: This 2017 tourism campaign, El Salvador Great Like Our People, was part of a national branding effort to counter global narratives of violence by highlighting Salvadoran hospitality, culture, and natural beauty (El Salvador Marca País, 2017). The advertisement emphasizes pride, warmth, and resilience as defining features of Salvadoran identity.

In response to negative portrayals, a government campaign such as El Salvador Great Like Our People sought to rebrand the nation through hospitality, culture, and natural beauty (El Salvador Marca País, 2017). Stuart Hall explains how texts encode pride and resilience, presenting Salvadorans as welcoming and hardworking people (Hall, 1980). But international audiences remain skeptical because they see the gap between promotional imagery and widely reported violence.

Barthes’ mythologies reveal how hospitality was naturalized as a timeless feature of Salvadoran identity (Barthes, 1972). Presenting culture and warmth as “natural” erases the historical struggles of migration and insecurity. Fraser’s framework builds on this, showing how branding silences counterpublics—particularly those who might raise concerns about power or inequality—to create an image of profitability for El Salvador (Fraser, 1990).

Marx’s commodity fetishism displays how pride and hospitality were commodified as tourism products within the advertisements (Marx, 1867). The campaign masked the social struggles behind the images, transforming national identity into a consumable brand tailored toward international travel. Using images of Salvadorans themselves enhances the image of what an “authentic” Salvadoran identity is, yet the advertisement continues to mask the lived reality of Salvadorans during that time.
