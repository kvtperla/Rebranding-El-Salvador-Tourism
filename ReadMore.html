Historical & Tourism Background of El Salvador

El Salvador Historic Background

The Salvadoran Civil War (1980–1992) was a twelve-year conflict between the El Salvadoran government, U.S.-backed military forces, and the leftist guerrilla coalition known as the Farabundo Martí National Liberation Front (FMLN) (Poveda, 2008). The war claimed an estimated 75,000 lives and displaced hundreds of thousands of Salvadorans, many of whom fled to North America and formed the Salvadoran diaspora. Although the Chapultepec Peace Accords of 1992 formally ended the war, the social and economic impact remained. Vanegas notes that El Salvador’s postwar economy struggled to attract investments, and tourism remained minimal for many years, overshadowed by instability and insecurity (Vanegas, 2012).

In the years following the war, gang violence increased. Groups such as MS‑13 and Barrio 18 spread throughout communities, gaining control in various cities. By the mid‑2010s, El Salvador had one of the highest homicide rates in the world, with 103 homicides per 100,000 inhabitants in 2015 (Poveda, 2008). International media coverage reinforced this narrative of extreme danger, discouraging tourism and limiting economic opportunities. Legeai’s documentary La Vida Loca exemplifies this period, portraying gang life as the defining feature of Salvadoran identity and amplifying the country’s negative global image (Legeai/Poveda, 2008).

Tourism campaigns during the 2000s and early 2010s attempted to counter this narrative. Government-led advertisements such as El Salvador Great Like Our People promoted culture, hospitality, and natural beauty (El Salvador Marca País, 2017). Latarola noted how surf tourism began to emerge as a niche attraction along the Pacific coast (Latarola, 2013). Yet these efforts were often overshadowed by international portrayals of violence. Paula and Eloy argue that national identity is often commodified through selective images to appeal to tourists (Paula & Eloy, 2013). El Salvador’s early campaigns followed this pattern, highlighting beaches and cultural pride while silencing the realities of poverty, migration, and insecurity.

A country long associated with war, diaspora, and gang violence set the stage for a dramatic rebranding under President Nayib Bukele. Since 2019, Bukele’s strategy has emphasized national security as the cornerstone of El Salvador’s new image, launching campaigns like Surf City and internationally showcasing his prison system as a symbol of safety. Bukele implemented mass incarceration policies and the Territorial Control Plan to enforce zero‑tolerance toward gang activity (LATAM FDI, 2023). During this period, El Salvador experienced a rise in tourism and foreign investment, with LATAM FDI reporting a “tourism and investment boom” tied directly to Bukele’s emphasis on safety and infrastructure. This shift demonstrates how El Salvador’s invisibility in global tourism was overwritten by a new narrative of pride and prosperity.

El Salvador Past Tourism Efforts

Vanegas examines tourism’s relationship to economic growth in El Salvador, arguing that tourism has the potential to stimulate development but has historically been undermined by weak branding and insecurity (Vanegas, 2012). Guaita Martinez et al. expand this idea by analyzing tourism competitiveness across Latin America, noting that countries with strong branding strategies and infrastructure investments attract more international visitors (Guaita Martinez et al., 2022). Compared to its regional neighbors, El Salvador ranked low in competitiveness during the early 2010s, reinforcing its negative image. Their analysis shows how branding and perception are crucial in shaping tourism outcomes.

Patel focuses on coastal tourism development, highlighting the uneven distribution of resources along Latin America’s Pacific coast (Patel, 2023). In El Salvador, surf tourism existed informally long before Bukele’s administration formalized Surf City. Latarola’s study supports this, showing how local communities marketed El Salvador’s beaches independently (Latarola, 2013). Reiser et al. argue that community involvement is essential for sustainable tourism development (Reiser et al., 2025). Their findings raise questions about whether Bukele’s top‑down branding strategy genuinely engages local communities or simply commodifies national identity for global consumption.

Paula and Eloy’s study of Mexico’s tourism branding provides a comparative lens, arguing that national identity is often commodified through selective imagery (Paula & Eloy, 2013). This parallels El Salvador’s campaigns, where advertisements like El Salvador Great Like Our People and Surf City highlight beaches and hospitality while downplaying poverty, migration, and violence. Gutierrez critiques such tourism efforts as forms of resource extraction, reshaping landscapes to serve global markets rather than local needs (Gutierrez, 2023). This critique helps contextualize Bukele’s campaigns, which prioritize international perception and investment while raising concerns about the lived realities that remain unaddressed.
