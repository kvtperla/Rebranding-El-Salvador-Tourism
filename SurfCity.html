Surf City (2019–present)

Short Description: Surf City is a national tourism initiative launched under the Bukele administration to rebrand El Salvador as a modern, safe, and globally competitive surf destination. The project focuses on coastal development, international surf events, and digital promotion to reshape the country’s global image and attract tourism.

Bukele’s Surf City initiative represents a more aggressive and globally visible rebranding strategy for the country (Nayib Bukele, 2025). In previous years, El Salvador had very few tourist locations, which made it difficult for the government to market itself online. Through investment in coastal infrastructure and promoting surfing as a global attraction, the Bukele administration was able to encode a new perspective of El Salvador (Hall, 1980). The campaign displays the country as modern, adventurous, and safe, hosting national and international surfing events (Nayib Bukele, 2025). For international tourists, Surf City is decoded as evidence of transformation, while Salvadorans may interpret it as selective storytelling that commodifies the beaches while ignoring the inequalities beneath them.

Barthes’ mythologies help explain how surfing is presented as a timeless symbol of Salvadoran identity (Barthes, 1972). This myth erases the historical reality that tourism had long been undermined by violence. Surf City constructs a dominant narrative of prosperity while silencing counterpublic concerns about displacement or environmental impact (Fraser, 1990). Terranova’s free labour framework is especially relevant here: Surf City heavily relies on digital promotion, with Salvadorans and tourists posting images and videos that amplify the campaign without compensation (Terranova, 2000). Their unpaid cultural labour blurs the line between government branding and organic enthusiasm. Surfing becomes commodified for both user and consumer, as media platforms continue to enhance this new identity of El Salvador.
