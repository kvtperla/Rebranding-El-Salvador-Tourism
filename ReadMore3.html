Theoretical Framework

Stuart Hall’s encoding/decoding model emphasizes that media texts are encoded with dominant meanings by producers but can be decoded differently by audiences depending on their social position (Hall, 1980). In El Salvador’s case, tourism campaigns encode messages of safety, prosperity, and cultural pride. Yet Salvadorans—especially those abroad—often decode these texts as selective propaganda, recognizing what is being left out. Hall’s framework highlights the gap between official narratives and lived realities, showing that meaning is never fixed but constantly negotiated.

Roland Barthes’ concept of mythologies builds on this by showing how cultural texts transform historical realities into myths that appear natural (Barthes, 1972). Tourism campaigns present beaches, hospitality, and increased safety as timeless features of Salvadoran identity. In doing so, they erase the country’s historical struggles and rebuild a new image of what it means to be Salvadoran. Safety itself becomes a myth—packaged as a product for tourists through selective imagery.

Nancy Fraser’s critique of the public sphere argues that dominant publics often silence counterpublic voices, particularly those of marginalized groups (Fraser, 1990). Bukele’s branding constructs a strong public narrative of national pride and transformation, but it ignores perspectives related to human rights abuses or inequality. Fraser’s framework deepens Hall’s idea of decoding tourism campaigns as propaganda—showing how advertisements can function as political tools that determine who gets to speak and whose voices are erased.

Tiziana Terranova’s idea of free labour highlights how digital platforms rely on unpaid cultural content created by users (Terranova, 2000). Salvadorans contribute to national rebranding by posting images, videos, and stories on social media. Their everyday content amplifies official campaigns, blurring the line between state propaganda and organic promotion. Terranova’s framework is especially relevant today, where national identity is co‑produced by governments and citizens.

Karl Marx’s concept of commodity fetishism frames tourism branding as a form of commodification. Marx argues that commodities hide the social relations that produce them (Marx, 1867). In El Salvador’s case, tourism branding transforms lived experiences—safety, pride, and identity—into consumable images for international audiences. This process hides the social struggles behind them, packaging national identity as a product.
